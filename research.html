Research Synopsis of research related activities

Motivation

Fluid-fluid interfaces play a dominant role in the behaviour of many industrial processes, in particular those that involve the coating (wetting) or separation (de-wetting) of particles. Examples of such processes include the extraction of bitumen from the tar sands, fabrication by ink-jet printing, drug delivery from micro- and nano-capsules, production of food such as ice cream and the stabilization of foams and emulsions. As such, developing a fundamental understanding of the underlying physics behind particles at fluid-fluid interfaces has obvious economic upside. Also, as environmental regulations around the world become stricter, there is pressure to move away from the use of traditional synthetic surfactants to control interface stability and substitute environmentally friendly alternatives. One such promising alternative is the use of biodegradable micro- or nano-particles that become trapped at the fluid-fluid interface. To date, the study of particles at fluid-fluid interfaces has been largely relegated to experiments. The increasing availability of computational resources, as well as improved numerical algorithms, makes the use of Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) another attractive alternative for a fundamental study of particles at fluid-fluid interfaces. The advantages of CFD include a high degree of control over simulation conditions, as well as the ability to extract any solution variables of interest from the domain with much greater ease than taking experimental measurements. Many algorithmic challenges exist, however, in particular the moving boundary problem for finite size particles. While attempts have been made to address this issue through the use of a re-meshing scheme, Immersed Boundary (IB) methods are another possibility for modelling the solid particles within the computational domain, which enjoy a considerable advantage in terms of time relative to re-meshing approaches.

Objective

The objective of this research is to investigate the interactions of particles near immiscible fluid-fluid interfaces, by developing a multiphase flow solver complete with a Direct Forcing (DF) immersed boundary method, which is capable of resolving the coupling between the flow solver and the solid body motion of the particle. The IB method to be used will be similar to Fluid-Structure Interaction (FSI) solver of Calderer et al., with the interfacial body forces computed in the manner proposed by Montazeri et al.. In addition to this, it is proposed to develop a fully coupled implicit framework such as those developed by Denner et al. and Darwish et al., rather than employ the standard approach to IB simulations where an un-coupled algorithm such as SIMPLE is used. The motivation behind this is to improve the coupling between body forces and the pressure field, which is key in reducing the magnitude of spurious currents in simulations with fluid-fluid interfaces. To the best of my knowledge, no such attempt to develop a fully-implicit, coupled solver for the solution of fluid-particle interactions at a fluid-fluid interfaces using an IB method has been made. In addition to the development of the solver, a number of applications will be investigated, in particular attempting to simulate various common experimental scenarios. The study of such experimental cases serves as both a method of validation for the numerical solver, as well as a method for studying fluid-particle interactions at fluid-fluid interfaces on scales that would be very difficult experimentally, perhaps providing new insight into the underlying physics.

Current Progress

To date, a fully three dimensional incompressible Navier-Stokes solver, similar to the framework proposed by Darwish et al. has been developed with immersed boundary capability. The immersed boundary method is based on a direct forcing approach similar to that proposed by Borazjani et al.. The framework, denoted as “Computational Algorithms Framework for Fluid Equations” (CAFFE), serves as a lightweight platform for the development and testing of immersed boundary approaches and numerical solvers. The solver uses a Dual Time-Stepping (DTS) scheme, with a Generalized Minimum RESidual (GMRES) iterative technique used to solve each of the sparse sets of linear equations arising from the momentum and pressure poisson equation at each inner iteration. In order to solve linear equations, the parallel GMRES solvers in the freely available PETSc library are used.

In addition to the development of the above framework, simulations conducted using OpenFOAM with immersed boundaries have been conducted for simple cases of flow around a cylinders/spheres. As the project progresses, development will be focused around the OpenFOAM framework, due to the broad availability of numerical tools within.
